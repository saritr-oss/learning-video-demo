Made a mistake? Don’t delete — void.

Errors can happen, even when processes are carefully followed. You might enter the wrong quantity, or associate the receipt with the wrong purchase order.

That’s why it’s important to remember: corrections must preserve the audit trail. Instead of deleting an entry, the proper action is to void it, ensuring that the system maintains a clear and transparent record of what occurred.

The Concept: In the immutable world of financial systems, we fundamentally never "delete" 
history as if it never happened. Such an action would fundamentally compromise the audit trail. 
Instead, we must "reverse" an erroneous transaction while preserving a transparent record of 



the original mistake and its correction. This action is crucial for maintaining a perfect audit trail 
and ensuring complete financial transparency. 


The Action: If you identify an error in a submitted receipt, locate that specific receipt in your 
history and precisely select "Void Receipt." 


The Result: This action does not erase the original line item. Instead, it generates a new, 
distinct document known as a "Void Consumption." This "Void Consumption" acts as a negative 
entry, mathematically zeroing out the ledger impact of the original erroneous transaction while 
critically preserving a transparent historical record that an error occurred and was 
systematically corrected. 


[Slide 7] 

So why do we use “void” instead of “delete”?

Because in financial systems, transparency is always more important than erasure. Every action must remain visible to preserve the integrity of the audit trail.

The Logic: As an architect of financial systems, you must choose between 'Deleting' and 
'Voiding' an erroneous entry. Before proceeding, outline your logical and ethical considerations 
for each action. Which action aligns best with the fundamental principles of transparency, 
auditability, and financial integrity, and why? 


Now, let's rigorously analyze the imperative behind "Void Consumption": Why do we introduce 
this seemingly more complex concept instead of a simple "Delete" function? Because robust 
financial systems demand a perfect, unalterable Audit Trail as their foundational principle. 


The Critique: 


● Deleting: This action fundamentally erases history. It obliterates any record of the original 
mistake, making it impossible for auditors or investigators to understand what transpired. If an 
auditor asks, "What happened here?", there is no traceable record, leading to significant 
compliance issues and a complete lack of transparency. 
● Voiding: Conversely, voiding meticulously preserves a complete record. It records both the 
original erroneous entry (e.g., +5 units) and the immediate, offsetting correction (e.g., -5 units). 
This creates an indelible, transparent historical narrative of the transaction, the error, and its 
systematic rectification. 
Key Term: Void Consumption. Internalize this term. It is the definitive sign of a transparent, 
compliant, and auditable correction process within any sophisticated financial system.

Let’s talk about the five-minute heartbeat.

This refers to the short integration gap between our systems — the time it takes for data to move from Coupa into SAP. Understanding this latency is important, because even a brief delay can have strategic and operational implications. 


After you meticulously submit the Goods Receipt (or a void transaction) in Coupa, you may be

The Gap: Coupa and SAP are inherently separate systems. They do not maintain a real-time, 



instantaneous sync. Instead, they operate on a periodic, scheduled integration cycle. 


The Rule: The interface connecting these two critical systems runs on a precisely defined 
5-Minute Cycle. 


● Instruction: It is a critical operational rule that you must wait at least 5 minutes after 
submitting any transaction in Coupa before attempting to verify its reflection in SAP. Failure to

false negatives and potential distrust in the system. You are waiting for the system to 
successfully generate a Material Document Number—a unique, SAP-specific identifier typically

Now let’s decode the SAP record.

In this step, we focus on validating the financial data carefully and systematically, ensuring that what appears in SAP accurately reflects the transaction and meets our rigorous verification standards. 


Inside the Purchase Order History tab, you will observe a grid of critical data. To ensure 
absolute accuracy and maintain financial integrity, you need to rigorously verify three specific 
columns. This process constitutes a vital part of our financial data validation protocol: 


1. MvT (Movement Type): Look specifically for the code "101." This is the standard SAP code 
universally assigned to a Goods Receipt, confirming the nature of the transaction. 
* *Why it's critical:* This code provides immediate confirmation that the transaction is 
indeed a goods receipt, preventing misclassification. 


2. Mat. Doc. (Material Document): This is the unique identification number systematically 
generated by SAP (e.g., 5000028491). It acts as the immutable identifier for this specific 
financial event. 
* *Why it's critical:* This unique ID is the definitive link to the financial record, essential for 
audit trails and tracing. 


3. Quantity: Verify that the amount listed in this column precisely matches what you initially 
entered in Coupa and, more importantly, what you physically counted. 
* *Why it's critical:* Any discrepancy here directly impacts inventory accuracy and financial 
liability, potentially leading to phantom inventory or under-accruals.

process automatically and permanently copies the original Coupa GR (Goods Receipt) Number
